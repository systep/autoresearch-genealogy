# Hypothesis: Peter Jungblut 1739 Naturalization Is a MARYLAND Record, Not Pennsylvania

## Statement

The 1739 colonial naturalization that established the legal status of Peter Jungblut/Youngblood and his children in British America was granted in **Maryland**, not Pennsylvania as originally conjectured. The governing record is Commission Book #82 of the Council of Maryland 1733-1773, which documents Peter Youngblood of Prince George's County, Maryland, a native of Germany, naturalized on 6 March 1738/9 (Julian calendar, equals 1739 Gregorian) together with his sons William and Peter and daughters Sarah and Mary. The original Palatinate village of origin is not stated in the extant record; only "Germany" (with Palatinate inferred from context) is given. Peter's first wife is unnamed and presumed deceased before the naturalization.

## Evidence For

1. **Commission Book #82, Council of Maryland 1733-1773** (cited in thegagenweb.com Youngblood migrations article and WikiTree Youngblood-199): Specifies Peter Youngblood of Prince George's County, native of Germany, naturalized 6 March 1738/9, with sons William, Peter and daughters Sarah, Mary. This is a Tier 1 primary colonial administrative record.
2. **Independent corroboration (Tier 2)**: Multiple independently-authored secondary sources converge on the same facts:
   - Youngblood DNA Project (FTDNA) background page: "Peter Jungblut/Youngblood who was naturalized along with his children in 1739 giving Palatinate, Germany as his homeland."
   - WikiTree Youngblood-199 profile (Peter Youngblood Sr. 1700-1770).
   - thegagenweb.com Youngblood migrations page (Emanuel-GA genealogy): specifies the Maryland Prince George's County setting, 1739 naturalization, and the children by name.
3. **Migration context**: German Marylanders timeline and scholarly articles (Wust, "Direct German Immigration to Maryland in the 18th Century"; Hennighausen, "Early German Settlements in Western Maryland") document the 1735 Palatine wave settling at Monocacy/Frederick via Annapolis and Alexandria, which fits Peter's residence pattern.
4. **Post-naturalization life events**: Peter remarried Mary Wheals on 26 November 1750 in Harford County, Maryland. Peter maintained personal records in German (secondary source). Youngbloods began disappearing from Maryland records circa 1745 and reappeared in Orange and Johnston Counties, North Carolina, as Peter Sr., Peter Jr., John, Thomas, Henry.

## Evidence Against / Gaps

1. **Specific Palatinate village unknown**: The extant record says only "Germany" or "Palatinate." Tier 1 village identification still missing.
2. **First wife's name unknown**: Naturalization record does not name her. No Maryland marriage record recovered yet for Peter's first marriage (likely occurred in Germany).
3. **Arrival ship unknown**: No Strassburger/Hinke Philadelphia passenger-list match located. Peter may have landed at Annapolis (records thinner) or arrived prior to the 1727 Philadelphia oath requirement (he was "in the disputed territory" by 1728).
4. **Original hypothesis error**: The session-note claim that the 1739 naturalization was in Pennsylvania is incorrect; the document is a Maryland Provincial Council commission, not a Pennsylvania Supreme Court or Nisi Prius naturalization. Pennsylvania Archives Second Series Vol. 2 covers 1740-1773 and would not contain a 1739 record in any case.

## Best Hypothesis for Palatinate Village of Origin

No primary source yet identifies the village. Candidate research leads, in descending probability:

1. **Rhineland-Palatinate Kurpfalz region**: Monocacy/Frederick MD Palatines largely originated from the Kurpfalz, Kraichgau, and Württemberg borderlands. Jungblut surname clusters historically in the Mosel and lower Rhine regions (Trier area, Hunsrück) and in the Nahe valley.
2. **Hunsrück / Mosel**: The surname Jungblut (also spelled Jungbluth, Jongebloet) is concentrated today in the Hunsrück-Mosel corridor (Rheinland-Pfalz, around Simmern, Kirchberg, Bernkastel-Kues). This is a strong candidate region given typical 1730s Palatine migration patterns.
3. **Nassau-Siegen or Westerwald**: Lesser-probability candidate based on surname distribution.

Without the original Palatinate parish or emigration record (required permit/manumission document in the sending jurisdiction), none of these can be elevated above speculative.
