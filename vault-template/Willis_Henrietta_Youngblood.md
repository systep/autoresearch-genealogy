## Biography

Willis Henrietta Youngblood was born November 23, 1863 in Columbus, Lowndes County, Mississippi. She was the daughter of **William Henry Youngblood** (b. May 1828, Bibb County, Alabama; d. March 24, 1916, Chattanooga, Tennessee) and **Esther Lucy Kyle** (b. July 1827, Alabama; d. May 20, 1912, Chattanooga, Tennessee). William H. Youngblood was a landowner in Lowndes County, Mississippi, having settled there by the 1850 census.

The **1880 Census** records the family in Beat 5, Lowndes, Mississippi: William Youngblood (age 50), wife Lucy (age 52), and daughters Viola (22), Eleanor (19), and Willie (17) (1880 US Census, Tier 1). Her brother Robert Kyle Youngblood was evidently already out of the household by this date.

Her sister **Viola E. Youngblood** (1857-1924) is documented on FamilySearch (ID LJ1H-YRB) and married a man surnamed Wert. Her sister **Eleanor Nellie Youngblood** married a man surnamed Drake. These married names are consistent with the family pattern of the Youngblood daughters marrying in Lowndes County or nearby before dispersing.

### Education and the "First Woman Graduate" Claim

The handwritten family chart notes Willis as the **"1st Woman Graduate of U.S. [?]"**, though this claim is ambiguous and almost certainly an exaggeration or garbled family tradition.

**Research findings (2026-04-13):**

The **Mississippi Industrial Institute and College** (II&C, now Mississippi University for Women) was chartered March 12, 1884 in Columbus, Mississippi, as the **first state-supported college for women in America**. Classes began October 22, 1885, with 341 students. The first ten graduates received their diplomas on **June 10, 1889**. Willis Youngblood would have been approximately 21-22 years old when classes began (1885) and 25-26 at the first graduation (1889), making her plausible as either a student or early graduate.

Columbus, Mississippi (Lowndes County) is precisely where Willis grew up. The II&C was literally in her hometown. Her occupation was recorded as "college school teacher" at death, consistent with having earned a degree there.

The "first woman graduate" claim likely derives from one of these scenarios:
1. Willis was among the **first ten graduates** of the II&C (1889), which the family later inflated to "first woman graduate of the U.S."
2. Willis graduated in an early class (1890-1893, before her 1890 marriage or shortly after) and the family generalized the II&C's distinction as "first state college for women" into a personal superlative.
3. The notation referred to the II&C's institutional status (first state women's college) rather than to Willis personally.

For context: Catherine Brewer Benson received the first bachelor's degree awarded to a woman in the U.S. in 1840 from Georgia Female College (now Wesleyan College) in Macon, Georgia. Willis was not the first woman graduate in the U.S. in an absolute sense. However, **if she was among the II&C's Class of 1889, she was one of the first ten graduates of the first state-supported women's college in America**, which is a legitimate and notable distinction. Known members of the first class include Miriam "Minnie" Greene Paslay (1869-1932), who became a professor of Latin and Greek at the II&C.

**Status**: Speculative but plausible. The II&C archives (Beulah Culbertson Archives and Special Collections at MUW) hold scanned catalogs from 1889-90 and 1890-91 that would contain student and graduate lists. The "Meh Lady" yearbook did not begin until 1902. Checking the 1889-91 catalogs would definitively confirm or refute Willis's enrollment and graduation.

### Marriage and Family

Willis married **George Michael Bright** (1852-1939) on **September 18, 1890** in Lowndes, Mississippi (MyHeritage, Tier 3). This date, one year after the first II&C graduation, is consistent with Willis having completed her education before marriage. George was the son of Samuel Graham Bright (1825-1909) and Mary Jane Bernard (1829-1900), from the Greenville County, South Carolina Bright family of German Palatinate (Brecht) origin.

Willis and George had four sons:
1. **Bernard Kyle Bright** (b. June 17, 1892, Abingdon, Washington County, VA; d. December 16, 1978, Jonesborough, Washington County, TN). Married Velma Imogean Jamison. Son: Bernard Kyle Bright Jr. Confirmed on Ancestry and Geni with parents listed as George Michael Bright and Willie H. Youngblood.
2. **[[Albert_Graham_Bright]]** (b. August 19, 1893, Abingdon, VA; d. September 5, 1961, Delray Beach, FL). Married [[Rebecca_Moseley_Long]].
3. **William Wert Bright** (dates unknown; first name "Wert" possibly from Willis's sister Viola's married name Wert, suggesting a family connection).
4. **Arthur Wade Bright** (dates unknown).

### Residences

Willis's census residences document extensive mobility through Virginia and Tennessee:
- Lowndes County, Mississippi (1880, 1890)
- Abingdon, Washington County, Virginia (1900; sons born here 1892-1893)
- Roanoke, Virginia (1910)
- Johnson City, Tennessee (1920)
- Big Lick, Roanoke, Virginia (1930)
- Kingsport, Tennessee (1935)
- Rochester, New York (1940)

Willis died June 28, 1952 in Bristol, Virginia, at the approximate age of 88. Her occupation at the time of death was recorded as "college school teacher." She was buried at **High Street Cemetery**, Rocky Mount, Franklin County, Virginia. The cemetery is a historic burial park operated by the Town of Rocky Mount, containing burials of many of the town's founding families (Rocky Mount town website).

Through her son Albert Graham Bright's marriage to [[Rebecca_Moseley_Long]], Willis is the great grandmother of [[Mary_Elizabeth_Bright]] (m. [[Henry_Michael_Kascher]]) and great great grandmother of [[Kathryn_Ann_Kascher]] (m. [[Alan_Carl_Salmanson]]).
