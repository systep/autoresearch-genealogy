# Hypothesis: Biblical Chain from Adam to King David (Traditional Attribution)

## Statement

The vault's Davidic line (King_David, King_Solomon, King_Rehoboam, Zerubbabel) can be extended backward through the scriptural chain recorded in 1 Chronicles 1-2, Ruth 4:18-22, and Genesis 5 and 11, reaching from David through Jesse, Boaz, Perez, Judah, Jacob, Isaac, Abraham, and ultimately to Noah and Adam.

This chain is TIER 3 TRADITIONAL ATTRIBUTION. It is recorded in ancient religious texts but cannot be corroborated by any independent primary source. It is preserved here for completeness of the scriptural tradition only and must not be treated as documented genealogy.

## Tier and Confidence

- Tier: 3 (tertiary, religious tradition)
- Confidence: Speculative
- Per CLAUDE.md Guard 1: every addition must cite a source. The sources here are scriptural texts, not primary genealogical records.
- Per CLAUDE.md Guard 2: user-contributed trees (Geni curated Biblical Tree project) are explicitly not trusted without independent corroboration, and no such corroboration exists for any generation prior to David.
- Geni's own Biblical Tree project administrators state: "there are no known valid lines going all the way back to the Bible period" and such lines represent "questionable medieval genealogy."

## Evidence For

1. 1 Chronicles 1-2 explicitly records the father-son chain from Adam through Noah through Abraham through Judah through Perez to David as a single continuous lineage.
2. Ruth 4:18-22 independently corroborates the Perez-to-David segment (10 generations).
3. Matthew 1:1-6 and Luke 3:31-38 independently preserve the Abraham-to-David and Adam-to-David chains respectively.
4. Genesis 5 (antediluvian) and Genesis 11 (postdiluvian) give gapless father-son chains with ages.
5. Geni's Biblical Tree project (started January 2009) has curated profiles for essentially every named person in this chain with scriptural citations.

## Evidence Against

1. Geni curators publicly state: "there are no known valid lines going all the way back to the Bible period" and Adam-connected lines on Geni are flagged as "questionable medieval genealogy" to be removed.
2. No primary documentary source exists for any person before approximately David (ca. 1000 BCE). Earlier generations are preserved only in scripture.
3. The Masoretic Text, Septuagint, and Samaritan Pentateuch disagree on the ages in Genesis 5 and 11, often by exactly 100 years per generation, producing date ranges that differ by thousands of years overall.
4. Hebrew genealogies frequently telescope (skip generations), so "X begat Y" does not guarantee father-to-son succession.
5. CLAUDE.md Guard 6: records should not be merged on "same name, same dates" alone. Here we have name-only matches to scripture with no corroborating spouse/child/occupation/location records.

## Analysis

This chain is a well-attested religious tradition. It is the genealogical spine of both Jewish and Christian scripture and is reproduced across thousands of commentaries, curated Geni profiles, and WikiTree entries. For the purposes of this vault, its historical reliability decreases sharply as one moves backward:

- David to Perez (10 generations): plausible outline, corroborated across Ruth, 1 Chronicles, and Matthew; dating fits Israelite archaeology broadly.
- Perez to Abraham (4 generations): scriptural tradition only, consistent across texts.
- Abraham to Shem (8 generations): scriptural tradition only; Abraham's historicity itself is debated among scholars.
- Shem to Adam (9 generations): antediluvian and immediately postdiluvian; cannot be treated as historical genealogy.

The chain is included here as scriptural context for the vault's Davidic line, not as documented ancestry. No person files will be created for these generations. Any downstream descendant linking to this chain must carry the Speculative confidence tier and cite this hypothesis file.

## Decisive Record

None exists. This hypothesis cannot be proved or disproved by documentary research. It will remain open indefinitely as traditional attribution. It should be superseded only if primary-source evidence (e.g., a corroborating inscription for a named individual) emerges, which is not expected for any generation prior to David.
