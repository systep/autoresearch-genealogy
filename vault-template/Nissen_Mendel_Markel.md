## Biography

Nissen Mendel Markel is the earliest known ancestor of the Markel family. He is identified in the Yichus narrative (Tier 2, primary family document by Ruth Gold, Oct 9, 2011) as the father of **[[Dov_Markel]]** and the grandfather of **[[Heschel_Herman_Markel]]** (Heshel Markel; b. 1862; d. 1916).

The patrilineal line established by the Yichus narrative is: Nissen Mendel Markel to Dov Markel to Heshel Markel (Tier 2).

No dates, occupation, or other biographical details are provided for Nissen Mendel. The family was located in Vanyavitch (Waniowice), a village near Sambor in Galicia (now western Ukraine). It is unknown whether Nissen Mendel himself lived in Waniowice or in Sambor proper.

### Estimated Date Range

Based on generational analysis: Heshel Markel was born 1862 (Tier 2). Dov (Heshel's father) was likely born c. 1830 to 1840. Nissen Mendel (Dov's father) was likely born c. 1800 to 1820, possibly earlier. This places Nissen Mendel's active years in the first half of the 19th century. Austrian Jewish vital registration in Galicia began in 1789 but most surviving Jewish registers for the Sambor area date from the 1830s or later. Nissen Mendel's birth may predate the surviving register period, which would explain the absence of vital records.

### The Name "Nissen Mendel"

"Nissen" (Yiddish: Nisn) derives from Hebrew Nisan, the Jewish month during which Passover takes place, traditionally given to boys born in that month. "Mendel" is a Yiddish diminutive of Menachem. The double given name "Nissen Mendel" follows Ashkenazic naming conventions where two Hebrew/Yiddish names were paired. The surname "Markel" is documented in Alexander Beider's "A Dictionary of Jewish Surnames from Galicia" (Avotaynu, 2004); it derives from the Yiddish personal names Markl/Merkl, hypocoristic forms of Marcus/Markward, which were also used as vernacular equivalents of the Biblical name Mordecai.

### Waniowice/Vanyavitch Village

Waniowice is confirmed as a real village near Sambor in Galicia. A German genealogy forum (Ahnenforschung.net) discusses "Tarnawa nizn, Szandrowiec und Waniowice bei Sambor in Galizien," confirming the village's location within the Sambor administrative district. The 1910 Galician census (Galizien Zensus 1910) lists Waniowice among Galician localities. No specific Jewish community records for Waniowice were found; Jews in small villages typically belonged to the nearest town's kahal. Based on the Sambor kehilla structure (which had jurisdiction over Jews in surrounding villages), Jews of Waniowice most likely belonged to the **Sambor kahal**. The Sambor kehilla was led by a board of 12 parnassim elected annually, and had compulsory authority over all Jews in the town and surrounding villages.

### Sambor District Jewish Records

Austrian civil registration began in 1784, requiring clergy to record births, marriages, and deaths. Jewish registers in Galicia were maintained under Catholic supervision from 1789, though most surviving Jewish registers date from the 1830s or later. Sambor district Jewish vital records are held in two main archives:

1. **Central State Historical Archives of Ukraine in Lviv (TsDIAL)**: Fond 701, Series 1. Contains bound volumes of vital records, index books, and loose vital records. Gesher Galicia has completed and uploaded indexes for Sambor records to the All Galicia Database (free, searchable online).

2. **AGAD (Archiwum Glowne Akt Dawnych), Warsaw**: Fond 300, containing approximately 3,278 registers of Jewish metrical records from eastern Galicia, primarily covering 1877 to 1911. AGAD has made scans available of nearly all registers. In 1877, the Austrian government assigned 73 Administrative Districts to Galician towns; Sambor was designated as a Jewish Administrative Center where Jewish metrical records (births, deaths, marriages) were to be kept for Jews in the town and surrounding villages, including Waniowice.

FamilySearch has microfilmed Jewish records from TsDIAL. The collection "Ukraine, Lviv, Metrical Books, 1786 to 1937" (FamilySearch collection #4426190) contains records from the Lviv Oblast archives. Gesher Galicia's All Galicia Database is the primary free search tool for indexed Sambor records. JRI-Poland also has a Sambor town page.

### Isserles and Landau Family Connections (from Yichus)

The Yichus narrative mentions "Issarles and Landau" as family connections. On Geni.com:
- **Rabbi Moses Isserles (Rema, c. 1530 to 1572)**: 440 Isserles profiles on Geni. The Rema was a major Ashkenazic rabbi in Krakow, author of the Mappah (glosses on the Shulchan Aruch). His grandfather was Jehiel Luria, first Rabbi of Brisk. He studied under Rabbi Shalom Shachna in Lublin, alongside his relative Solomon Luria (Maharshal) and Chayyim ben Bezalel (brother of the Maharal of Prague).
- **Landau rabbinical family**: 12,656 Landau profiles on Geni. The most famous member is Rav Yechezkel Landau (the "Noda B'Yehudah," 1713 to 1793), born in Apta, Poland. Rabbi Moshe Landau served alongside the Rema in the Krakow Beit Din. Isaac Landau served as Rabbi of Krakow 1754 to 1769.

The Isserles and Landau families intermarried extensively with other major rabbinical dynasties (Horowitz, Jaffe, Luria, Katzenellenbogen). A claimed connection to these families from a Galician Jewish family like the Markels is plausible but unverified. Many Galician Jewish families claimed (sometimes correctly, sometimes not) descent from the Rema or other major rabbinical figures. Without specific genealogical documentation of the link, this remains **speculative (Tier 3)**.

### Drohobycz-Boryslaw Markel Family Page

**Status**: The website drohobycz-boryslaw.org has a Markel family page describing the same Lutowiska family (Sara Hinde Markel + Shamay Bartel, 1877). The grandmother's brothers lived in Lwow and emigrated to Israel in 1936, bringing their parents to Israel in 1939. The grandmother's sister Rebeka married Efroim Wilf from Boryslaw and had four sons: Moses, Dyonyo, Juda, and Herman. During WWII, family members were deported to the Sambor ghetto and killed at Belzec extermination camp (November 1942). In 1930, Matilda (the second daughter) married a farmer from the village of Olszanik, near Sambor. **Relationship to Waniowice Markels: unestablished.** The Lutowiska Markels and Waniowice Markels were in the same broader Sambor region, and the surname "Markel" is relatively uncommon. Further investigation could determine if these are branches of the same extended family.

### Waniowice Civil Registry

Waniowice, as a small village, did not have its own civil registry (Standesamt). Under the 1877 Austrian system, Jewish vital records for Jews in Waniowice and other small villages were kept at the **Sambor Jewish Administrative Center**. All birth, marriage, and death records for the Markel family of Waniowice would be found in the **Sambor Jewish metrical books**, held at TsDIAL Fond 701 (Lviv) and AGAD Fond 300 (Warsaw).
