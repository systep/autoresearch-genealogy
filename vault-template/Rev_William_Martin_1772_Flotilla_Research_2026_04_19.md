# Hypothesis: Robert King II sailed on one of Rev. William Martin's five 1772 ships

## Claim Under Test

Robert King II (b. c. 1756, Ireland; d. 1826, Anderson County SC) sailed from Ulster to Charleston in 1772 as part of Rev. William Martin's Reformed Presbyterian (Covenanter) migration, on one of the following five ships. Sub-claim: the specific ship can be identified from surviving records.

## The Five Ships (Round 6 Corroboration, 2026-04-19)

Synthesised from Discover Ulster-Scots, Boyd Roots, Penny Wind, immigrantships.net (ISTG Vol 16), Causeway Coastal Route, and cross-checked against Jean Stephenson, Scotch-Irish Migration to South Carolina, 1772.

| Ship | Tonnage | Master | Agent | Departure Port | Departed | Arrived Charleston | Families | Persons |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| James and Mary | 220 | J. Workman | | Larne | 25 Aug 1772 | 18 Oct 1772 (quarantined Sullivan's Is. for 7+ weeks, smallpox, 5 children died) | ~50 | ~150 |
| Lord Dunluce | 400 | James Gillis | John Montgomery | Larne | 4 Oct 1772 | 20 Dec 1772 (Rev. William Martin on board) | 184 | 394 |
| Pennsylvania Farmer | 350 | | | Belfast | 16 Oct 1772 | 19 Dec 1772 | 86 | 215 |
| Hopewell | 250 | J. Ash (or Martin) | William Beatty | Belfast | 19 Oct 1772 | 22 Dec 1772 | 63 | 186 |

Aggregate: approximately 438 families, 1,177 persons. Jean Stephenson's published figure cites 467 families and about 1,000 passengers, consistent with the above within expected manifest gaps.

## Passenger List Provenance

Per Discover Ulster-Scots and Boyd Roots: **no original complete manifests survive**. Heads of household reconstruction is possible from two sources:

1. **Belfast News-Letter, 22 December 1772**: published letters from the James and Mary and the Lord Dunluce passengers after arrival, naming satisfied passengers (approximate list pages 30-31, 35, 42-101 of transcription at burval.net and ulsterancestry.com).
2. **South Carolina Council Journal, January 6 1773 onward**: land petitions by passengers, bounty grants of 100 acres (self) + 50 acres (spouse) + 50 acres (per child). Indexed in Stephenson, and in part on the Discover Ulster-Scots PDF (discoverulsterscots.com/sites/default/files/documents/2021-05/1772 Passenger List.pdf), which includes columns SHIP, FIRST NAME, SURNAME, AMOUNT OF ACRES, LOCATION.

## Geographic Mismatch Problem

A newly surfaced problem for this hypothesis: the Martin flotilla families overwhelmingly settled in **Newberry, Abbeville, and Chester Counties**, SC (Boyd Roots; minerdescent.com on Reformed Presbyterian settlement at Rocky Creek, Chester Co). Rev. Martin himself settled at Rocky Creek, Chester Co, where he built a stone house.

Robert King II is documented in **Anderson County (Pendleton District)**, which is separated by roughly 100 miles to the west and is not a Martin flotilla settlement cluster. This geographic mismatch weakens the Martin flotilla hypothesis.

Two reconciliations are possible:

- **A**: Robert King II was on a Martin ship but moved westward from Chester/Abbeville to Pendleton within the first 5-10 years after arrival (before 1780 Battle of Kings Mountain service). Intra-backcountry migration of this distance was common.
- **B**: Robert King II was not on a Martin ship at all, and arrived in SC via a different route (independent Ulster sailing 1770-1772, overland from Pennsylvania via Great Wagon Road, or overland from Charleston after an earlier arrival).

## Revised Sub-Hypotheses, Ranked

### H-1a: Lord Dunluce (Larne, departed 4 Oct 1772)

Strongest candidate if Robert King II was on a Martin ship. Reasons: (i) departed Larne (the stated family tradition port); (ii) carried Rev. Martin and the largest passenger contingent (394 persons); (iii) published Belfast News-Letter 22 Dec 1772 list is searchable; (iv) associated bounty land grants are in the SC Council Journal Jan 1773. Confidence: **Moderate**, pending Stephenson + PDF check.

### H-1b: James and Mary (Larne, departed 25 Aug 1772)

Second strongest. Same Larne origin as family tradition. Earlier arrival (Oct 1772), but passengers quarantined off Sullivan's Island until early December due to smallpox, meaning effective settlement date is comparable to Lord Dunluce passengers. Confidence: **Moderate**, pending list check.

### H-1c: Hopewell, Pennsylvania Farmer, or Free Mason

Lower probability because these sailed from Belfast or Newry, not Larne. But passenger movement between Antrim sailing ports was common, and the "Larne" family tradition may be a post-hoc fossilisation of the most famous Antrim port, so cannot be fully excluded. Confidence: **Speculative**.

### H-2: Not on any Martin ship

Robert King II's Anderson County settlement and the Martin flotilla's Chester/Abbeville/Newberry settlement pattern are geographically discordant. The family may have arrived via an unrelated 1770-1772 Ulster sailing (e.g. one of the Belfast or Derry to Philadelphia voyages), overland to SC. Confidence: **Moderate, requires explicit elimination before confirming any H-1**.

5. **Pull SC Council Journal January 1773 land petitions** (SC Dept of Archives and History). Filter for King surname. A King bounty grant of 100 acres would be decisive for H-1. A King bounty grant located in Chester / Abbeville / Newberry (not Anderson / Pendleton) would further specify which ship.
6. **Check Anderson / Pendleton County land grants 1772-1785** for Robert King. If his earliest SC grant is in Anderson and is not a 1773 Martin-bounty grant, H-2 strengthens.
7. **Test H-2 explicitly**: check Philadelphia, Baltimore, and Charleston 1770-1772 arrivals independently of the Martin flotilla; check Pennsylvania western migration records along the Great Wagon Road.
8. **DNA triangulation**: if any descendant of Robert King II has tested and matches descendants of known Martin flotilla passengers on Chester / Abbeville lines at IBD segment level, that is an independent Tier 2 corroboration.
