# Yiddish Postcard ("Carte Postale") — Markel Family Correspondence

## Image Provenance

This vault note describes a chat-shared image — a postcard back ("Carte Postale" watermark printed in Latin script across the top center, characteristic of French-issue postcard stock used internationally from the early 1900s) covered in handwritten **Yiddish cursive** in dark ink. The image is currently chat-only (not pushed to `Assets/`); reserved filename when uploaded: `Yiddish_Postcard_Carte_Postale.jpg`.

## Physical Description

- Postcard format. The back of the card is divided by a faint vertical line down the center — standard postcard layout for separating message (left half) from address (right half), used after the international Postal Union format change of 1907.
- The text fills both halves of the back (message has overflowed into the address area), suggesting a long message and that the addressing was done elsewhere (perhaps on the front, indicating a picture postcard) or on a wrapper.
- Ink: dark blue / black, fountain pen or steel-nib pen.
- Light pinkish stain at upper right (possibly age-related discoloration or contamination).
- Approximately **9 lines** of text visible, written in Hebrew/Yiddish cursive.

## Script Analysis

- The script is **Yiddish handwritten cursive (handgeshribene yiddishe kursiv)**, not Hebrew block letters. This is the everyday handwriting style of Eastern European Jews ~1880-1939.
- Letter forms are consistent with **Galician / Polish Yiddish** script tradition; many letters have the rounded loops typical of Lemberg / Lwów / Kraków region writers.
- No clear date stamp, postmark, or stamp visible on the back side of the rendered image.
- No salutation line in Latin script; no signature in Latin script.

I will not produce a speculative full transcription, since per CLAUDE.md Guard Rail #1 (do not fabricate) it is more harmful than helpful to invent words for letters I cannot read.

What I *can* observe with reasonable confidence:

- A possible name or place reference midway through the text (the cluster of capital-style letters that often signals a proper noun in Yiddish handwriting). The cluster *could* read like "...בארג" *...berg* (a common Galician town-name suffix, e.g., Lemberg). **NOT confirmed.**
- The line lengths and density suggest a personal letter rather than a formal communication.
