# Hypothesis: Vault Youngblood Family Descends from Palatine German Jungblut Immigrant via Pennsylvania and Edgefield SC

## Statement

The vault's Youngblood cluster (William H., William Henry, Eleanor Nellie, Viola E., Willie H., Willis Henrietta, Robert Kyle, Robert Youngblood Wert, James Youngblood, and associated kin), located in the SC/GA Southern US region, most likely descends from Peter Jungblut (also rendered Jungbluth, Youngblood), a Palatine German who arrived in Pennsylvania prior to 1728, was naturalized in 1739 with his children, and whose descendants migrated south via the Great Wagon Road into Edgefield County, South Carolina, in the 1750s-1770s. A secondary possibility is descent from the John Miles Youngblood (b. 1707/1708, Baltimore County, Maryland) line, which is YDNA-distinct from the Edgefield Palatine cluster.

## Evidence For

1. **Peter Jungblut 1739 PA naturalization (WikiTree, Youngblood FTDNA project)**: Primary-era record shows a Palatine-origin Peter Jungblut naturalized with children in 1739, explicitly declaring Germany (some accounts specify Palatinate) as homeland. Multiple SC Peter Youngbloods (Peter c.1725-1796 of Edgefield; Peter Jr. c.1732-1788; Peter c.1710-1795 of Charleston) are YDNA-close and trace to this immigrant.
2. **Migration pathway documented (Edgefield County Historical Society; Piedmont Trails on Great Wagon Road)**: The Great Wagon Road was the principal PA-to-Carolina-to-GA migration route from the 1740s to 1770s; Edgefield received a wave of settlers from PA, VA, and NC between 1756 and 1766. This matches the Jungblut/Youngblood movement from Philadelphia area to SC backcountry.
3. **Independent DNA grouping (FamilyTreeDNA Youngbloods of Edgefield SC project)**: Multiple Southern Youngblood lines cluster together genetically and separate from the earlier Maryland John Miles line, supporting a single Palatine progenitor for the SC/GA cluster.
4. **Surname linguistic evidence**: Jungblut, literally "young blood" in German, is the direct source anglicization. Typical Pennsylvania Dutch communities produced this translation-style surname change.
5. **Joseph Youngblood, Revolutionary War officer in SC Militia**, and James Youngblood of Edgefield (1790 census) confirm Youngblood presence in Edgefield County by the Revolutionary period.

## Evidence Against

1. **Not all colonial Youngbloods share a progenitor**: The YDNA project notes pre-1700 Youngbloods are genetically distinct from the post-1710 Palatine group; John Miles Youngblood (1707/08, MD) appears to represent a separate earlier line.
2. **Peter c.1686 ancestry unresolved**: The generation above the 1739-naturalized Peter is poorly documented; whether Peter c.1686 was a distinct immigrant or a conflation is unsettled (WikiTree notes "massive uncertainty").
3. **Vault lacks a documented bridge**: No current vault person file cites a primary source connecting any vault Youngblood (William H., James, etc.) directly to Peter Jungblut or to the Edgefield Peter. The link remains an inference based on geography and surname cluster.

## Analysis

The evidence strongly supports a Palatine German origin for the SC/GA Youngblood cluster via Pennsylvania, reaching Edgefield County through Great Wagon Road migration in the third quarter of the 18th century. Two independent secondary-tier sources (WikiTree collaborative genealogy and the Youngblood FTDNA project) agree on Peter Jungblut (naturalized PA, 1739) as the progenitor of the Edgefield line, and the migration route is historically well documented. Confidence at the surname-cluster level is Moderate (per project confidence tiers: multiple secondary sources agree, YDNA supports).

Confidence that any specific vault Youngblood (e.g., William H. Youngblood, James Youngblood) descends from this immigrant specifically is currently Speculative: the geographic and temporal alignment is suggestive, but no primary-source chain of custody has been established in the vault. The vault's James Youngblood may or may not be the James Youngblood (c.1740-1792) of Edgefield; name and era align, but this must be verified against the vault's known birth/death dates, spouse, and children before claiming identity.

Secondary alternate hypothesis: if vault Youngbloods descend from the Maryland John Miles line (1707/08 baptism at St. George Parish, Baltimore County), the PA German Palatine hypothesis is refuted and the origin would instead be English or earlier German (via Thomas Youngblood, Baltimore County tax lists 1702).

## Decisive Record

1. The 1739 Pennsylvania naturalization record for Peter Jungblut (available in PA colonial naturalization compilations and Strassburger/Hinke, Pennsylvania German Pioneers) listing his children by name.

4. YDNA test results from a living male-line Youngblood descendant matched against the FTDNA Youngbloods of Edgefield project (would confirm cluster membership).
